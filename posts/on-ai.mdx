## Concessions
AI, ultimately, is the most powerful tool humanity has ever created.
It is and will continue to be the magnum opus of mankind.
It will create breakthroughs in each and every aspect of science, math, engineering, medicine, and countless other subjects.
Its dominance is inevitable.
I'm not going to pretend that I don't use AI. 
I don't like to use it, but it is a necessity to survive in what is being called the Fourth Industrial Revolution. 
I'm not going to be the caveman that doesn't acknowledge the advent of fire. 
I use ChatGPT for day-to-day questions, Claude for larger projects, and NotebookLM to create study guides and summarize large amounts of data.
It is soon to be, if not already, a requirement to be literate in AI tools to land a job in the modern labor market.
It's already having an impact, even on those who do adapt. Goldman Sachs estimates that up to 300 million jobs will be affected by AI [1].
Just last year alone AI replaced 16000 jobs per month [2]. 

I initially was a big supporter of AI, especially when I was in high school.
Here was a novel new technology that would help me write essays, solve math problems, and do [almost](https://www.reddit.com/r/LocalLLaMA/comments/1mqlqij/ai_censorship_is_getting_out_of_handand_its_only/) anything I asked it.
I thought it was amazing, and I still do! It is incredible what you can accomplish with AI in a fraction of the time that would normally be spent.
However, as time goes on, I become less and less fascinated with AI and more and more terrified. 

> How long is it before my job gets replaced by AI?

This question has rung in my head throughout my college career, as I find many peers of mine relying solely on AI for homework assignments and projects. 
While it can be a helpful tool to check work, it is scary how many copy down what it produces, unchecked. I think back to my first few years of high school, when I was confident I wanted to study computer science or something in the tech field. 
Now, computer engineers face some of the highest unemployment rates out of college compared to any other degree [3]. 
I can only imagine it getting worse as the vibe-coding trend continues to grow.

In the end, I can dislike AI however much I want, but it is still a tool that we all must learn how to use.
However, I think it is important to discuss the effects of AI on the brain, the earth, and society, even after a few short years of its prominence. 
## A Second Brain (?)
In January 2025, Michael Gerlich published an article on the effects of AI on the brain through statistical analysis [4]. He found that there is a "significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities."
Gerlich also writes that 
> "excessive reliance on external aids may lead to a decline in internal cognitive abilities, such as memory retention and critical analysis skills ... it may undermine the development and maintenance of critical cognitive skills".

Not only is AI damaging our neural capabilities, but it is also rewiring the rewards system of the brain. In June of 2025, MIT ran a study to explore the effects of AI on the brain [5].
They had 54 subjects among different age groups divided into 3 groups and asked them to write a SAT essay using ChatGPT, Google Search, or nothing at all.
They found that participants who wrote essays with the help of LLMs felt that their papers carried lesser value and did not feel as accomplished.
They wrote that 
> "participants who were in the Brain-only group reported higher satisfaction and demonstrated higher brain connectivity."

There have been additional studies on how the usage of AI effects neuroplasticity, the brain's ability to form new neural connections. Neuroplasticity is something that needs to be maintained through consistent usage, something AI takes away. Rossi, Fraccaro, and Manzotti [6] wrote that 
> "networks' connectivity… is weakened if subjects rely exclusively on AI help." 

All of these studies also show hope for the brain, stating that these effects all vary based on how a person engages with AI tools.
If people passively engage with AI and take it as fact, they suffer the effects I highlighted.
People who actively engage with AI don't show these trends, with some studies [4, 5] finding that it can actually help with developing these skills.
However, I have observed through my academic career that many people blindly follow AI.
This isn't just from me watching classmates or overhearing conversations. I have worked as a grading assistant for a class I took and I have seen many students turn in work that is completely wrong.
Sometimes this is just an anomaly, but then I notice a few other students did it the same way and got the wrong answer.
I type the question into ChatGPT, and it gives the same wrong solution that all of these students are getting. 
People are blindly following ChatGPT without questioning it, and it's bigger than just academia.
We're already seeing the early impacts of technology on brain development through Generation Alpha, and that's been mostly without AI so far. 
> The greater population is beginning to treat AI as a second brain, and it's harming their first one.

We're already seeing the onset of a much larger literacy crisis due to children being raised exclusively on smartphones and other devices. I'm terrified to see what will happen to the next generation.
It is imperative that we learn how to properly integrate these tools into the education system so that they are being used to nourish brain development and critical thinking skills.

If humans are becoming increasingly reliant on the "second brain" that is AI, we'd better hope it has the same quality as ours.
It doesn't. We're already seeing a decline in quality of AI. The IEEE published an article [9] discussing how the quality of AI coding has seen a degradation, even offering more "insidious method[s] of failure."
It creates flaws that are very difficult to detect unless looked very meticulously. 

What I believe is currently the main cause of the decline in AI quality is the fact that it is training on synthetic data, i.e. text or images generated by AI.
Graphite [10], an SEO company, found that
> "the quantity of AI-generated articles has surpassed the quantity of human-written articles being published on the web."

All of the AI companies have web crawlers that prowl the internet for any new piece of text to be consumed into the training model.
In turn, they are also scraping AI-generated media. Of course, there are detection systems put in place to avoid feeding AI-generated content into the training models.
However, even if AI detects that an article or piece of media is AI generated, there is not a 100% success rate in this automated detection.
Anthropic [11] writes that "as few as 250 malicious documents can produce a 'backdoor' vulnerability in a large language model."
Over the millions of pages and images that AI trains off of, this small number of synthetic documents can easily be achieved even with an extremely small error rate.
AI will inevitably become worse as it begins to train on the output it produces, becoming an incestuous amalgamation of fiction and reality.
> AI is eating itself, just like an Ouroboros.

Even if this isn't the main cause now, it will be. An article posted in Nature found that AI models collapse when trained on synthetic data produced by other models.
They say that "indiscriminately learning from data produced by other models causes 'model collapse'— a degenerative process" [12].
Additionally, they demonstrated that "this process is inevitable, even for cases with ideal conditions."
For reference, they define model collapse as a "degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative models, in which the data they generate end up polluting the training set of the next generation."
Models that train on this polluted data end up "mis-perceiv[ing] reality."
They even confirm my theory from earlier: "it is unclear how content generated by LLMs can be tracked at scale."
This is no longer just a theory, but now an impending reality. 
## The Need to Mean Something
In the modern digital economy, the data we leave behind is worth more than we are.
To quote Watch Dogs 2, "you are now less valuable than the data you produce."
This is especially true in the age of AI, where every word of every book, every blog post, every reddit thread is all fed into the eternally unsatiated machines that are the AI training models.
Every tap, every behavior, is tracked and fed into algorithms that use artificial intelligence to keep you scrolling and engaged to generate even more data.
The younger generations are striving to break free.
There is a shift towards analog technology as we crave the intimacy of physical media.
People are starting a new rebellion against the content machine.
In a survey of 2,000 Americans, half say they are intentionally disconnecting from the digital world, with more Gen Z and Millennials being offline than older generations [7].
People would rather engage with non-digital tools and be slower and less efficient than use the cutting-edge technology available at our fingertips. What does this say about the need to mean something? 
> In the modern world, all we have to leave behind is a trail of data.

After we die, what is left? Pictures on our phone, posts on Instagram, Facebook?
As AI becomes smarter and smarter, there is less that sets humans apart besides what is tangible.
I've began embracing this analog lifestyle myself.
I recently bought a physical camera separate from my phone to take pictures and I've began listening to vinyls and CDs more than music streaming services.
I crave something real, and I'm not the only one. The digital world leaves much to be desired.
An email doesn't carry the same weight as a physical, handwritten note, and not even the best filters can replace the authenticity of a Polaroid picture or a picture taken from a retro digital camera.
This resurgence of analog offers a glimmer of hope in an increasingly digital world.
Nostalgia has become a commodity, and it's in high demand. 

## What's the Rush?
Drew Gooden is a commentary youtuber who made a very captivating video on AI and the state of it on the internet [8].
I highly recommend watching it because I think he provides a very valuable insight into the AI craze, specifically relating to artwork and creativity.
Something he said in this video that specifically stood out to me is
> "Part of what makes art special is that it's difficult to make, even with all the tools right in front of you... it would take me like 10 years to learn how to make ... that. Okay. That's fine, what's the rush?"

What is the rush? This stood out to me because of how much it aligns with what I've been saying.
Part of creating or working really hard on something is the reward of seeing the culmination of your hard work.
Think back to what I said earlier about how AI rewires our rewards system in our brain.
People feel more accomplished and better about themselves when they do something entirely themself.
How long before this is no longer an option? It's already becoming an inevitability in the workplace.
If you don't adapt, you'll be left in the dust.
Something Drew said in his video that resonates with me on a personal level is
> "Call me stubborn, but I'd rather make something on my own than watch a computer make something good."

Humans have been improving through massive struggle for years and where we are now and what we know is a result of that.
Once you remove the reward for the struggle (the feeling of accomplishment) what will happen to progression?
Will we solely rely on AI for progression? What does a world where humans have no motivation to progress because AI is doing everything look like?
That's not something I want to find out. I think that's why people are going back to analog activities. 
The effort is worth the reward and we have been trained to crave that feeling over millions of years of evolution. 
## Environmental Considerations
Besides neurological and sociologic impacts, there is an environmental impact that has real, physical impacts on the world.
Currently, the electricity is the biggest factor, but water consumption plays a role too.
Chilled water is used to cool data centers, and it is estimated that for each kilowatt-hour of energy a data center needs, 2 liters of water are consumed for cooling [13].
By the end of this year, electricity consumption by data centers is expected to reach 1050 terawatt-hours.
This means that water usage is expected to reach over 2 trillion liters of water per year.
Noman Bashir, a Computing and Climate Impact Fellow at MIT, says that 
> "[t]he demand for new data centers cannot be met in a sustainable way."

This large amount of power consumption also indirectly increases water consumption.
Bloomberg News found that approximately two-thirds of new data centers are built in places already under high levels of water stress [14].
Additionally, water cooling doesn't currently use grey water or salt water or any other alternatives.
It uses, clean, potable, water. Any engineer can tell you about the complications of water cooling if the water is contaminated, especially in the evaporative cooling models that data centers employ. 
Any contaminant, whether that be salt, heavy metals, detergents, oils, (the latter two found in grey water) will be deposited and left in the piping, creating a plethora of problems.
There are currently efforts to use grey water in water-cooling systems, but why would the big AI data centers care?
Potable water is cheaper and easier to use, and there aren't any regulations limiting them right now.
It's already having an effect on people. Meta recently built a $750 million data center in Newton County, Ga.[15].
Soon after, local residents reported issues with their water supply.
The Morris family, who live 1,000 feet away from the data center, say that within a year of construction beginning, their entire water supply had dried up.
They couldn't use their dishwasher, their laundry machine, or even their kitchen sink. The entire county is facing this problem. The county is expected to face a water deficit by 2030, with water rates increasing by 33% year-over-year, compared to the typical 2%.
Meta claims innocence, but Georgians weren't having these problems before they moved in.
This is happening all over the world, not just the United States.
Newsha Ajami, a hydrologist from Stanford acknowledges that, to these companies, "Water is an afterthought" [15].
There are even more horror stories from the residents of Georgia in the article.
I'd recommend reading them and afterwards questioning how great AI really is. 
## Conclusion
Like I said in my introduction, AI is inevitable.
We all must learn how to use it and employ it in daily tasks, even outside of the workplace.
The world changes too fast to intentionally ignore breakthroughs in technology.
Currently, however, the world is changing too fast and I think I've done a good job of highlighting that.
The machine of progress is an unstoppable force, but I think it will soon meet the immovable object that is the stubbornness of humanity.
